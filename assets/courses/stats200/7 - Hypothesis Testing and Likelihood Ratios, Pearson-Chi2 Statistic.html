Lecture 16, 17, 18, 19 Hypothesis Testing and Likelihood Ratios, Pearson-Chi2 Statistic

Summary:
Use the likehood ratio to do hypothesis tests. If the hypotheses are simple hypotheses (that is, the parameter is a value, not a range), then we can just do a simple likelihood ratio comparison.

If either the null or alternate hypothesis is a composite hypothesis (the parameter belongs to a range of values), you have to go a generalized likelihood ratio test, which is changing the numerator or denominator of the likelihood ratio to use the maximum likelihood over the range in question.

For 1 sided tests, the simple likelihood ratio comparisons are often enough, since oftentimes the hypothesis test is uniformly most powerful, that is, applicable to all alternate hypothesis. If this not the case, you have to do generalized likelihood ratio for the range of values in the alternate hypothesis.

For 2 sided tests, do generalized likelihood ratio tests.

The Nyman-Pearson Lemma states that the likelihood-ratio hypothesis test is the test with the most power for any level of significance α.

Notes:

  • Often discrete distributions are hard to get exact Λ, take asymptotic estimates for discrete distributions.
  • If we reject when Λ<k and Λ is monotonic in X¯, we can reject when X¯<k′ for some other k′, this is often a useful trick.
  • For the generalized likelihood ratio , we use −2logΛ∼χ2df as our test statistic, where df = # of params estimaated in denominator - # of params estimaated in numerator

Hypothesis Testing Recap

Suppose we are choosing between two hypotheses
Ho=p=0.5
Ha=p=0.7
for the probability p of a weighted coin. Suppose out of 20 flips, we get 18 heads. The likelihood ratio of our two hypotheses is
(2018)0.5180.52(2018)0.7180.32.
We have a decision matrix:

------ p=0.5 reality p=0.7 reality
p=0.5 hypothesis Correct Type II Err
p=0.7 hypothesis Type I Err Correct

  • The decision rule is d(x)={10ts>tnts≤tn, where ts is a test statistic and tn is a threshold.

  • The significance level of a test p P(d(x)=1|H0 is true)=α=P(Type I Error)
    (Type I being null is true but we reject it for the alternative; false negative).

  • The power of a test is P(d(x)=1|H0 is false) = β = 1−P(Type II Error).
    The probability of rejecting if the alternative is in fact true. The probability of our test forcing a change if it is in fact needed.

If the distribution of the hypothesis parameter is fully specified (i.e. p ~ Some distribution) this is a simple hypothesis, if we are unsure about the distribution this is a composite hypothesis.

Nyman-Pearson Lemma

(Essentially says to use likelihood ratios to test against two hypotheses)

Given two simple hypotheses H0,H1, we define the likelihood ratio k=f0(x)f1(x), where f0, f1 are the likelihoods of the data given hypotheis 0 or 1.

Given sample data x∗=x∗1,...,x∗n to test our hypothesis, if we use a rule which rejects the null if f0(x∗)f1(x∗)<k and accepts the null if f0(x∗)f1(x∗)≥k, this is the rule which has maximal power for any fixed level of significance α. [Proof in Lecture 16 Lecutre Notes]

One Sided Tests

Ex) Normal Distribution
Given X1,...,Xn∼iidN(μ,σ2);f(xi)=12πσ2√e−12(x−μσ)2.
H0:μ=μ0
H1:μ=μ1(μ1>μ0)
We find a test statistic which is the likelihood ratio Λ.

Λ=exp(−12σ2(∑ixi−μ0)2)exp(−12σ2(∑ixi−μ1)2)

We can drop the scaling term from the normal pdf above because it doesn't depend on μ and cancels out above and below.
We need to find the distribution of the test statistic Λ under H0. We reject when Λ<k, accept when Λ>k, and we wish to find k.
We note that Λ is monotonically decreasing in X¯ (Appendix 1), so we can rewrite our decision rule in terms of X¯ and some other k′ (which we will just call k for simplicity). We wish to find what the new k is.

We set α=P(Reject H0|H0 true)=P(X¯>k|μ=μ0).
Use X¯∼N(μ0,σ2/n) given X∼N(μ0,σ2).
Then, α=P(X¯−μ0σ/n√>k−μ0σ/n√|μ=μ0), so 1−α=Φ(k−μ0σ/n√), which means that k=μ0+σn√Φ−1(1−α). We go out and get data, compute X¯, and then accept or reject base on its value with respect to k.

Power:
The power of this test is

1−β=P(H0rejected|H0false)=P(X¯>μ0+σn‾√Φ−1(1−α)|μ=μ1)=P(X¯−μ1σ/n‾√>1σ/n‾√(μ0+σn‾√Φ−1(1−α)−μ1)|μ=μ1)1−β=1−Φ(X¯−μ1σ/n√+Φ−1(1−α))

Note: Uniformly Most Powerful Tests: The above test that this doesn't depend on μ1 at all - it is a uniformly powerful test. It could equally be well used to test the composite hypothesis H0:μ=μ0;Ha:μ>μ0.

Ex) Poisson Distribution
In the Poisson Distribution, we have to use the central limit theorem to get an approximate confidence interval.

Given X1,...,Xn∼iidPoisson(λ);f(xi)=e−λλxixi!.
H0:λ=λ0
H1:λ=λ1(λ1>λ0)

Then the likelihood ratio is

Λ=e−λλ∑ixi/∏ixi!e−λλ∑ixi/∏ixi!

Λ=en(λ1−λ0)(λ0λ1)nX¯

As in the Normal case, this is a monotonic function of X¯. So we can use X¯ as our statistic and find a k to use as threshold.

It is hard to use X¯ to find k because our distribution is discrete. Instead we will use the asymptotic distribution of X¯, which is normal because of the Central Limit Theorem: X¯∼N(λ0,λ0/n).

We set α=P(Reject H0|H0 true)=P(X¯<k|H0) = P(X¯−λ0λ0/n√<k−λ0λ0/n√|H0).
This is ≈Φ(k−λ0λ0/n√), so k=λ0+λ0/n‾‾‾‾√Φ−1(α).
As in the above case, this does not depend on λ1, so it is a uniformly most powerful statistic, and we could also use it to test for H0:λ=λ0;H1:λ<λ0

Two Sided Tests

The Generalized Likelihood Ratio

Define the Generalized Likelihood Ratio:
ΛG=Λ=Likelihood of X given H0Maximum Likelihood of X givenH1

The generalized likelihood ratio ΛG is used when our alternative hypothesis H1 is a range of values (not just one value), and the simple likelihood ratio is not uniformly most powerful. In this case, we take the maximum likelihood of X given the range of H1 to serve as denominator. Also note that if the null hypothesis is also composite, we have to take the max likelihood of both the numerator and the denominator.

Use chi-squared to test generalized likelihoo ratio: Generally, we use −2logΛG as our test statistic, and compare it to a χ2df,0.05 statistic to perform the test. This works for normal and non-normal distributions because of the Central Limit Theorem. Note that the degrees of freedom = # of params estimated in denominator - # of params estimated in numerator.
Below we simply use Λ to denote ΛG for simplicity.

Ex 3: Multinomial Model, Testing Whether or Not Hardy-Weinburg structure Holds
Suppose we have three values x1, x2, and x3 drawn from a multinomial distribution (balls and urns), where either
H0: Hardy-Weinburg structure -
The probabilities have a Hardy-Weinburg structure (The probabilities are parameterized by a single parameter θ, and are each in turn x1:(1−θ)2, x2:2θ(1−θ), x3:θ2.
H1: Unstructured -
There is no condition on the probability of each urn (The probabilities of each urn is p1,p2,p3,∑ipi=1 with no other constraints)

How do we test this hypothesis? Notice that both the null and the alternate hypotheses are composite hypotheses, so we have to take the max of both the numerator and the denominator of our likelihood ratio. We have

Λ=maxθ[(1−θ)2x1][2θ(1−θ)]x2θ2x3maxp1,p2,p3:∑ipi=1px11px22px33

We have to take the max likelihood of both the num and the denom.

DENOM: the max likelihood estimators are p̂ 1=x1/n,p̂ 2=x2/n,p̂ 3=x3/n. We plug it in and get (x1/n)x1(x2/n)x2(x3/n)x3 This is computable.

NUM: we take the log likelihood and it becomes a simple equation in x1,x2,x3 and θ. So this is also computable.
Thus Λ is comutable.

Note that degrees of freedom = # of params estimated in denominator - # of params estimated in numerator = 2 - 1 = 1. (Denom is 2 because the third is fully determined by the first two).
Finally, we take −2logΛ>χ21,0.005 to test.

The Pearson χ2 Statistic

Recall that in the multinomial case Λ=p̃ x11p̃ x22⋯p̃ xkkp̂ x11p̂ x22⋯p̂ xkk, where p̂ k=xk/n for some p̃ k depending on the hypothesis to test.
The −2logΛ=2∑ki=1xilog(p̂ kp̃ k)=2∑ki=1xilog(nxk/nnp̃ k).
If we call Oi=xi the observed count and Ei=np̃ i the expected count, this is equal to 2∑ki=1Oilog(OiEi)∼χ2.

The Pearson χ2 Statistic is an alternative χ2 statistic which performs the same function.
Def. The Pearson χ2 Statistic is defined as \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i} \sim \chi^2 $. A proof of this approx equivalence is in Appendix 2
