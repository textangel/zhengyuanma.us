Sufficient Statistics

Suppose X1,...,Xn∼f(⋅|θ).
Can we find a statistic T=f(X1,...,Xn) that captures all the information we need from X1,...,Xn to compute θ?
Def: Sufficient A statistic T=f(X1,...,Xn) is sufficient for X1,...,Xn∼f(⋅|θ) if f(X1,...,Xn|T=t) does not depent on θ for any value of t.

Ex) Bernoulli-
Given, X1,...,Xn∼iidBer(p);f(xi)=P(Xi=xi)=pxi(1−p)1−xi;xi=0,1.
We test if T=∑ixi is a sufficient statistic.
Check if P(X1=x1,...,Xn=n|T=t) doesn't depent on p.

P(X1=x1,...,Xn=n|T=t)=P(X1=x1,...,Xn=n,T=t)P(X1=x1,...,Xn=n)=P(X1=x1,...,Xn=n,T=t)(nt)pt(1−p)n−t=pt(1−p)n−t(nt)pt(1−p)n−t=1(nt)

This doesn't depend on p so T=∑ixi is sufficient.

Factorization Theorem To Generate Sufficient Statistics
Suppose x1,...,xn∼iidf(⋅|θ).
T is a sufficient statistic if and only if f can be written f(x1,...,xn;θ)=g(T(x1,...,xn),θ)⋅h(x1,...,xn).
That is, if you can take your density function and write it in the form above, by factoring out all the terms without θ from all the terms with θ then T is a sufficient statistic. (Proof in Appendix 1).

Rao Blackwell Theorem

Given X1,...,Xn∼f(⋅|θ).
Suppose that T(x1,...,xn) is a sufficent statistic for theta, and θ̂  is an estimator for θ which does not depend on T. Define another estimator θ̃ =E[θ̂ |T]. Then, E[(θ̃ −θ)2]≤E[(θ̂ −θ)2], that is, θ̃  is an improved estimator. (Simple Proof in Appendix 2)

In other words, if you have an estimator that doesn't depend on the sufficient statistic, you can take it's expectation with respect to the sufficient statistic and and get an even better estimator. Sometimes one can easily take a very crude estimator $g(x), and evaluate the conditional expected value to get an estimator which is in many ways optimal.

Aside: The Exponential Family This is the origin of one of the greatest unifying theories in statistics, the exponential family.

  • Normal, Gamma (χ2, Exponential), Beta, Multinomial, Poisson, Binomial distributions are all members of the exponential family.

Appendix 2: Proof of Rao Blackwell Theorem

E[θ̃ ]=E[E[θ̂ |T]]=E[θ̂ ] so the two estimators have the same bias.
So instead of comparing the MSEs (E[(θ̃ −θ)2] and E[(θ̂ −θ)2]), we can just compare the variances.
Var(θ̃ )=Var(E[θ̂ |T])
Var(θ̂ )=Var(E[θ̂ |T])+E[Var(θ̂ |T)] by the law of total variance.
Therefore, Var(θ̂ ) indeed ≥Var(θ̃ ).
