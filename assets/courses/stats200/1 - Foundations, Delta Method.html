Addition Rules

  • Cov(a+X,Y)=Cov(X,Y) by Cov(a+X,Y)=E[(a+x−E[a+X])(Y−E[Y])]=E[(X−E[X])(Y−E[Y])]=Cov(X,Y)
  • Cov(aX,bY)=abCov(X,Y) by
    Cov(aX,bY)=E[(ax−aE[X])(bY−bE[Y])]=E[ab(x−E[X])(Y−E[Y])]=abCov(X,Y)
  • Cov(X,Y+Z)=Cov(X,Y)+Cov(X,Z) by
    Cov(X,Y+Z)=E[(X−E[X])[(Y−E[Y])+(Z−E[Z])]]
    =E[(X−E[X])(Y−E[Y])]+E[(X−E[X])(Z−E[Z])]=Cov(X,Y)+Cov(X,Z)
  • Putting it all together, Cov(aW+bX,cY+dZ)=acCov(W,Y)+bcCov(X,Y)+adCov(W,Z)+bdCov(X,Z)
    or more generally, if U=a+∑biXi, V=c+∑djXj, then
    Cov(U,V)=∑i∑jbidjCov(Xi,Yj).
  • Applying the above to Var[X]=Cov(X,X),
    • Var(X+Y)=Cov(X+Y,X+Y)=Var(X)+Var(Y)+2Cov(X,Y)
    • Var(a+∑biXi)=∑i∑jbibjCov(Xi,Xj).
    • Var(∑iXi)=∑iVar(Xi) if the Xi are independent.

Bias and Variance

Delta Method aka Method of Propagation of error

Suppose we have some random variable X, and know the first two moments (mean and variance). Suppose we wish to compute some statistic about some function Y=f(X).
We can do this by the Taylor approximation (Appendix 1). We apprximate

Y=f(x)≈f(μX)+(x−μX)f′(μx)+12(x−μX)2f″(μx)+⋯

Using the first order approximation, we obtain μY≈f(μX),σ2Y≈σ2X[f′(μX)]2.
If we take the second-order Taylor expansion, we can get a closer approximation μY≈f(μX)+12σ2Xf″(μx)

Bivariate Delta Method

(This section refers to some results, in particlar the formulas for Var(X¯),Var(Y¯) and Cov(X¯,Y¯) in Lecture 2 - Simple Sampling.)
Suppose we have a random variable R=f(X,Y) which depends on two other random variables X and Y, and we know the moments of X and Y.
However, statistics such as E[R], Var[R] are really hard to compute. We have to use the bivariate delta method to approximate it.

General framework: How do we compute a statistic of f(X,Y)? We can use the 2D Taylor Expansion (the first order given here):
f(X,Y)≈f(μX,μY)+(∂f∂X(μX,μY))(X−μX)+(∂f∂Y(μX,μY))(Y−μY)+⋯

Example:
Suppose we were interested in a ratio of means between two populations both of size N: r:=μyμx=∑Ni=1yi∑Ni=1xi. We perform simple random sampling to estimate this value - so our estimator is R=μyμx=∑ni=1Yi∑ni=1Xi=Y¯X¯. What is the variance of R?
In this case, for m=X¯ and n=Y¯,

  • f(m,n)=n/m,
  • a(m,n)=∂f∂m(m,n)=−nm2,
  • b(m,n)=∂f∂n(m,n)=−1m.

By Taylor Expansion, f(X¯,Y¯)=f+a⋅(X¯−μX)+b⋅(Y¯−μY) (remember that f,a,b are function values)..

Computing the approximate estimator mean
To get a more accurate estimator, we'll use the second order expansion.
R=f(X¯,Y¯)≈f(μx,μy)+fx(μX,μy)(X¯−μx)+fy(μX,μy)(Y¯−μy)+1/2⋅fxx(μx,μy)(X¯−μx)2+1/2⋅fyy(μx,μy)(Y¯−μy)2+fxy(μx,μy)(X¯−μx)(Y¯−μy)
Taking the expectation of both sides,
E[R]=R+0+0+1/2⋅fxxVar(X¯)+1/2⋅fyyVar(Y¯)+fxyCov(X¯,Y¯)
The bias in the estimator is every term above except for the first. fxx,fyy,fxy can be easily obtained from partial differentiation, and Var(X¯),Var(Y¯),Cov(X¯,Y¯) were omputed in the section in stratified sampling.

Computing the approximate estimator variance
We simply use the first order Taylor expansion.
Var(R)≈Var(f+a(X¯−μX)+b(Y¯−μY))=a2Var(X¯)+b2Var(Y¯)+2abCov(X¯,Y¯)

By a similar procedure as in the variance derivation in "Simple Sampling" Lecture we can compute:

  • Cov(X¯,Y¯)=σxynN−nN−1, where σxy is the population covariance between x and y.
  • a=−μyμ2x=−rμx
  • b=−1μx

and let ρ be the correlation coefficient- that is, σxy=1N∑i(Xi−μx)(yi−μy), and ρ=σxyσxσy.

Pytting all this together with the above, we have (no need to memorize this formula!)

Var(R)Var(R)≈r2μ2xVar(X¯)+1μ2xVar(Y¯)−2rμ2xσxynN−nN−1=r2μ2xσ2XnN−nN−1+1μ2xσ2YnN−nN−1−2rμ2xσxynN−nN−1≈1n1μ2xN−nN−1(r2σ2X+σ2Y−2rσxy)

Aside
We could have used x/y or y/x as our statistic. When is one preferred to another? Let cx=σxμx be the "coefficient of variance for x". Then we prefer y/x to x/y when ρ>cx2cy, where ρ is the population correlation coefficient.
