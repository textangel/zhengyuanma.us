Parameter Estimation

Assume we have a functional form for our random process, that is, we already know what family of distributions it is in. The task of estimation is to find estimates for the parameters within that functional form.
Possible methods:

  1. Method of moments
  2. Maximum Likelihood
  3. Linear Regression / Least Squares
  4. General Optimization ("Machine Learning")

Once we generate the parameters by some recipes, the estimates have properties that we need to test.

  1. Standard error (aka standard deviation of the estimate)
  2. Bias (is E[θ̂ n]=θ]?
  3. Consistency - Does teh estimator's params converge to the actual params in some ense? Generally we want P(|θ̂ n−θ>δ|)→0 as n→∞∀δ>0. In this case we say θ̂ n is consistent and write θ̂ n→pθ.
  4. Sampling distribution Sometimes we can get an explicit formula, sometimes we have to use asymptotics (if parameter estimates are function of normal variable parameters), sometimes we can simulate the distribution via bootstrapping or Monte Carlo).

Method of Moments

Suppose we have a data distribution where we know its parametric class, and we want to find its parameters. We can sample from the distribution many points and compute as many moments as we need (most parametric families only require 2, or 3 moments), and backcompute what the parameters should be using the equations of the parametric class.

Method of moments always gives us a consistent estimator. However, it does not always give us an unbiased estimator.

Moments

A the kth mmoment of a random variable is the expected value of Xk.
Population Version: Define the n-th moment:
μk=E[Xk]=∫xkp(x)dx

Sample Version: Given a sample from a population X1,...,Xn, let
μ̂ 1=E[X]=1n∑Xi
μ̂ k=E[Xk]=1n∑Xki

The Method of Moments:

  1. Generate samples from a population and compute sample moments
  2. Use the parametric family's equations to compute what the population moments have to be.

Ex 2) Poisson distribution
Given a Poisson distribution Xi,...,Xn∼Po(x), such that P(X=x)=e−λλxx! for x=0,1,..., compute a MOM estimator for λ and find its properties.
[Note: Poisson has mean λ and variance λ]

popl moment E[x]=λ=sample moment=1n∑i=1nXi

We use λ̂ =1n∑ni=1Xi as our estimator. This is sum of Poisson random variables and is itself a Poisson random variable. ∑Xi∼Po(nλ), so λ̂ ∼1nPo(nλ). What is the expectation and variance of our estimator?

  • E[λ̂ ]=1nE[Po[nλ]]=1bnλ=λ.
  • Var[λ̂ ]=λn by Var[λ̂ ]=1n2Var[Po[nλ]]=nλn2=λn

Ex 3) Normal Distribution
Suppose X1,X2,...,Xn∼N(μ,σ2).
Then,
E[X]=X¯=μ̂ 
E[X2]=μ̂ 2+σ̂ 2=1n∑iX2i , so
σ̂ 2=1n∑iX2i−(1n∑iXi)2

Note: In solving MOM problems you can always use the fact that μ and σ2 are independent, that is, X¯ and 1n∑i(Xi−X¯)2 are independent.

After solving, we find that for a normal distribution, the estimator for the mean is normal (μ,σ2n) and variance is χ2 with n−1 degrees of freedom.

  • μ̂ ∼N(μ,σ2n)

  • σ̂ 2∼σ2nχ2n−1

    Bias Analysis:
    E[σ2]=E[σ2nχ2n−1]=σ2n−1n. Note that the MOM estimator for σ2 is not unbiased! To make it unbiases we generally divide by 1/(n−1) instead of 1/n.

Aside:
It is hard to know what the sampling distribution is just by looking at the above parameters! This is a problem. There are several solutions to this:
Aymptotics: We can try to understanding what r̂ , p̂  are using aymptotics. μ1, μ2 are approximately normal, so we can use properties of the normal distribution to get asymptotic properties of r̂ , p̂ .
Simulation: We can simulate our sampling distribution using the bootstrap. (Appendix 1)

Appendices

Appendix 1: The BootStrap

Invented by my advisor Bradley Efron!
Suppose you have a data sample X1,...,Xn. We can perform nonparametric boostrapping to generate the sampling distrbuion of r̂  and b̂ .

To perform boostrapping, we sample n values x11,x12,...,x1n with replacement. With our sample, compute μ̂ 1,μ̂ 2, and then compute r̂ ,p̂  based on that. Perform this sampling a large number B times, to obtain B estimates of r̂ ,p̂ .
Finally, we set r̂ , b̂  to be the mean of our distributions for each parameter from the bootstrap.
To get the confidence interval, find the empirical bootstrap distribution and get the 2.5 and 97.5th percentiles.

There is also parametric bootstrapping: Use the estimates for r̂ ,p̂  and generate n random samples X11,X12,...x1n from a perfect negative binomial distribution. You will get estimates with less than B if you are right in your assumptions that the process is very close to bonomial.
